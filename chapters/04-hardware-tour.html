Chapter 4

Top panel anatomy

Every control on the MK1 faceplate, numbered to match the official manual. By the end of this chapter no key on the surface should be unfamiliar.

The diagram below uses the same numbering as the official Octatrack User Manual, OS 1.40A. Numbers 1–9 cover knobs, the LCD, and indicator LEDs. Numbers 10–22 cover the function keys, navigation, parameter row, and transport. Numbers 23–26 cover the scenes, crossfader, and time-signature cluster. The 16 trig keys at the bottom are not individually numbered in the official scheme — they're treated as a single control (the bottom band), with secondary labelling reproduced verbatim.

Knobs, the screen, indicator LEDs (1–9)

1 · Headphones volume control

Top-left knob, marked Volume on the faceplate. Sets the level at the rear-panel headphones jack. On the MK1 it is dedicated to the headphones output and does not affect the main outs — track levels are set in the Mixer page (per-track) and the master output is set with FUNCTION+LEVEL (callout 7).

2 · Audio Record LEDs (A – B and C – D)

Two horizontal rows of small LEDs to the right of the volume knob, labelled A — B and C — D. They show the signal strength on the corresponding rear-panel input pair. Pre-fader: they indicate what's arriving at the converters, not what the recording-setup gain stage is doing with it. Use the rightmost (red) LED as a clip indicator and back the source off if it lights on peaks.

A second function: when the Recording Setup menu is open, these LEDs also indicate the source selection for the recorder trigs — a useful confirmation that the recorder is listening to the source you expect.

3 · Audio Record buttons (REC AB / REC CD)

The two large round red buttons. There is one record button per audio input pair: the left one captures pair AB, the right one captures pair CD. Pressed alone (in the standard PERSONALIZE configuration) they begin sampling immediately into the active recorder buffer. With FUNCTION they open the corresponding Recording Setup page; with a TRACK key held they record into that specific track's recorder.

4 · Track buttons (T1–T8)

The eight small round buttons flanking the LCD: T1 through T4 in a column to the left of the screen, T5 through T8 in a column to the right. Each has a track-status LED beside it.

The official manual's description, in summary:

  • Press a TRACK button to focus on that track.
  • TRACK+AUDIO RECORD records into that track's recorder.
  • FUNCTION+TRACK mutes the selected track.
  • CUE+TRACK routes the selected track to the cue bus.

The track LEDs show three states simultaneously: which track is the focus, which tracks are muted, and which tracks are routed to cue. Their colour and brightness convey the combination — the full table is in Appendix A's LED-state legend.

5 · LCD

128 × 64 pixel monochrome graphical interface. The MK1's screen is fixed- contrast — there is no backlight intensity control. Cleaning: a microfibre cloth and very mild isopropyl. The display has been observed to develop horizontal banding on units beyond a decade old; this is cosmetic and does not affect function.

The screen divides into three regions: the title bar (active track number, mode indicator, transport state, BPM), the main pane (large knob arcs and parameter values), and the label strip at the bottom (six four-letter parameter labels mapping to data-entry knobs A–F). When a parameter is locked on the active step, the corresponding label inverts.

6 · Card Status LED

A small LED near the top edge of the panel, beside the Compact Flash label. Lights during card activity (read or write). If it stays lit continuously for more than a few seconds without action, the OT is busy with a long save or a scan; do not power-cycle until it returns to dark.

7 · LEVEL

The larger grey knob to the right of the LCD. Sets the overall volume level of the active track (post-FX, pre-crossfader). Its function changes by context:

  • Normal play: active-track level.
  • FUNCTION+LEVEL: main output volume — the closest thing to a master fader the OT has on the front panel.
  • With a TRIG held in grid edit: sets the trig's conditional value.
  • In TEMPO mode: sets project tempo.

8 · TEMPO

Single button on the right side, between the encoder cluster and the scene controls. Pressed alone it brings up the Tempo menu. The TEMPO LED above the button flashes at the current BPM — so even with the menu closed, the panel tells you the tempo at a glance. Tap-tempo: hold FUNCTION and tap TEMPO repeatedly.

9 · Data-entry knobs (A B C / D E F)

Six endless encoders in a 2×3 grid: A B C in the top row, D E F in the bottom row. Each one edits whichever parameter the LCD's bottom label strip currently assigns to it. Push any knob while turning to scroll in coarse increments — useful for jumping past large value ranges. Pushing without turning is ignored by most pages; a few sub-screens use it as enter.

The function block (10–18)

10 · MIXER

Opens the Mixer menu, where input/output settings, per-track levels, and the cue/solo states are accessed. FUNCTION+MIXER opens the Project menu — the topmost level of the OT's data hierarchy, where you save, load, format, and switch projects.

11 · MIDI

Toggles MIDI editing mode. When the MIDI LED is lit, the eight track keys select MIDI tracks rather than audio tracks; the parameter row's labels change to NOTE, ARP, LFO, CTRL 1, CTRL 2; and the encoders edit MIDI parameters.

When the Recording Setup menu is open, the MIDI LED becomes a second-purpose indicator: it shows the signal strength of the internal sample source as well as which internal source is selected for the recorder trigs.

FUNCTION+MIDI opens the Parts menu. When a Pickup machine is loaded on the active track, the same combination performs a number of Pickup-specific commands (covered in chapter 25).

12 · FUNCTION

The universal modifier. Held with any other key it accesses that key's secondary action. Secondary labels are printed on the faceplate in red, beneath or beside their primary labels. Treat FUNCTION as the OT's Shift key; once you've memorised twenty or so combos, the panel feels much more compact than it looks.

13 · CUE

CUE+TRACK routes that track to the CUE outputs; its primary purpose is headphone pre-listen, with a secondary use as an independent stereo pair when Studio Mode is enabled (see chapter 5, callout 10). FUNCTION+CUE becomes RELOAD — restores the active part's parameters from the saved state, useful as a safety net before destructive edits.

14 · PATTERN

Held alone, turns the trig row into a 16-pattern selector for the active bank. Releasing without pressing a trig dismisses the selector without changing the pattern. FUNCTION+PATTERN opens the Arranger.

15 · BANK

Held alone, turns the trig row into a 16-bank selector (A–P). Each bank holds 16 patterns and 4 parts. FUNCTION+BANK opens EDIT mode — the per-pattern edit menu.

16 · ENTER · YES

Round button beneath the parameter row, with an LED indicator above it. Confirms menu actions. Has a long tail of secondary uses across other contexts: TRIG+YES previews a step (with all parameter locks) on the MAIN bus; TRACK PARAM+YES randomises the active page's parameters (an OS 1.40 addition).

17 · EXIT · NO

Round button beneath ENTER. Cancels menu actions, exits sub-screens. Mirrors the secondary actions of YES with the inverse semantic: TRACK PARAM+NO reloads the active page's parameters from the saved part — together with YES it functions as a per-page A/B comparison. FUNCTION+NO erases parameter locks at the playhead during live record.

18 · Arrow keys

Cluster of four small round buttons in a + arrangement: ▲ ▼ ◀ ▶. Cursor navigation in menus, parameter scaling on some pages, and sequencer page-turn (LEFT and RIGHT cycle through the four pages of a 64-step pattern). With FUNCTION: LEFT and RIGHT shift trigs left or right within the page — a quick way to reorganise a beat without redrawing it.

Parameter row, transport (19–22)

19 · Parameter pages

Five rectangular buttons in a row above the transport. Each one selects a parameter page that the data-entry knobs A–F then edit. On audio tracks: PLAYBACK, AMP, LFO, EFFECT 1, EFFECT 2. On MIDI tracks (with the MIDI LED lit): NOTE, ARP, LFO, CTRL 1, CTRL 2.

The SETUP band beneath the row indicates the secondary state: holding FUNCTION while pressing any of these five opens that page's setup screen — where, for example, you assign which sample slot a track plays from, or which effect occupies the FX2 slot.

20 · COPY (REC)

The transport REC button. Primary function: arms recording into the sequencer. REC+PLAY enters live-record mode; adding FUNCTION enters quantised live-record. Secondary function (with FUNCTION): COPY — the universal copy-to-clipboard operator, contextual to whatever is currently held (steps, patterns, parts, scenes, samples, slots).

21 · CLEAR (PLAY)

The transport PLAY button. Primary function: starts and stops the sequencer. Secondary function: CLEAR — the universal clear/empty operator, contextual to whatever is held. FUNCTION+PLAY additionally resets the sequencer to step 1 before starting.

22 · PASTE (STOP)

The transport STOP button. Stops the sequencer; pressed twice in quick succession also silences any active reverb and delay tails immediately. Secondary function: PASTE — the universal paste-from-clipboard operator. Together, the Copy/Clear/Paste triad is the OT's standard editing toolkit.

Scenes, crossfader, scale (23–26)

23 · SCENE A · MUTE

Round button at the lower-right area, to the left of the crossfader. Held alone, it turns the trig row into a 16-scene selector for assigning which scene the A end of the crossfader maps to. Note that SCENE A is an assigner, not a switcher — it sets which scene the fader's A end controls; it does not jump scenes. With FUNCTION it becomes MUTE for its assigned scene — useful for instant bypass without moving the fader.

24 · CROSSFADER

The horizontal slider between the two scene buttons. Morphs between the two scenes assigned to its A and B ends. Every parameter on the device that supports a scene lock can be morphed by it.

On MK1 the crossfader is an optical sensor riding a mechanical track; on MK2 it is contactless. The mechanical nature of MK1's fader means it can develop dust-related crackle over time — curable with a careful disassembly and a clean (covered in Appendix G's troubleshooting tree).

Note · the crossfader's actual behaviour

Newcomers often expect the crossfader to behave like a DJ-mixer crossfader — fading between two stems on its A and B ends. The Octatrack's fader does not work that way. It morphs between two parameter snapshots; whether those snapshots correspond to "two different mixes" depends entirely on what you locked into Scene A and Scene B.

25 · SCENE B · MUTE

Mirror of Scene A on the right side of the crossfader. Assigns which scene the fader's B end maps to. With FUNCTION: mute Scene B.

26 · SCALE SETUP

Small button at the far right with four LEDs (1:4 2:4 3:4 4:4) above it indicating the current step's relationship to the master scale. Held alone (or pressed) it opens the per-track length, master length, and time-scale settings — covered in chapter 17.

The play surface — 16 trig keys

The bottom row of the panel is sixteen large round buttons numbered 1–16, each with an LED above it and a dual label below: a letter (A–P) and a track number (T1–T8, repeated twice across the row). Trigs 1–8 are grouped under Track Trigs; trigs 9–16 under Sample / MIDI Trigs.

Their primary function depends on the active trig mode (chapter 18):

  • TRACKS mode — drop sequencer trigs on the active track's pattern.
  • CHROMATIC — play the active sample at twelve different pitches across the row.
  • SLICES — trigger the active sample's slice points (up to 64 slices, paged across the trig row).
  • SLOTS — switch the active track's sample slot.
  • QUICK MUTE — instant per-track mute toggling.
  • DELAY CONTROL — temporary delay-send modulator.

The letters A–P are referenced when BANK is held (each bank is named by its letter); the track numbers T1–T8 are used in MIDI mode for direct track addressing from external keyboards.

Backlighting and labelling on MK1 vs MK2

The MK1's keys are not individually backlit — only the per-track and per-trig LEDs above the buttons illuminate. Secondary functions (the FUNCTION+key actions) are silkscreen-printed in red on the faceplate, in small type, and are quite easy to miss. The button-combo encyclopedia in Appendix A reproduces every label; photocopying that appendix and keeping it next to the unit while you learn is a perfectly reasonable thing to do.

The MK2 adds a separate row of dedicated track-select keys, an ARR button, a PROJ button, an AED button (audio editor), a MIX button, and a third record button (REC 3) that captures internal sources directly. On MK1 each of these is reached by a combination of the buttons described above; the relevant combos are noted at the start of each chapter where they apply.

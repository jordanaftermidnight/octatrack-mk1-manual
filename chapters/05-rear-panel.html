Rear panel & connections

Twelve connector groups along the back edge. Numbering matches sec. 3.2 of the official OS 1.40A manual.

1 · Power on / off

Rocker switch. Always stop the sequencer and save your project before powering down — the Octatrack does not commit unsaved RAM-resident recordings to the CF card on power loss. Power-cycling while a save is in progress can also corrupt the project file.

2 · 6V DC power in

Centre-positive 6 V / 3 A barrel jack — the ⊖ — ⊕ symbol next to the jack confirms the polarity. Use the original Elektron PSU or a high-quality replacement matching the polarity and current rating. Underspecified supplies cause intermittent reboots, especially on busy projects.

3 · Hi-Speed USB 2.0

Two functions only: mass storage for accessing the Compact Flash card from a host computer (PROJECT → SYSTEM → USB DISK MODE), and SysEx transport for OS upgrades and backup dumps. There is no USB MIDI, no USB audio, and no Overbridge — the port has never carried audio in either direction, on either MK1 or MK2.

4 · Compact Flash card reader

Type I CF slot. The MK1 was originally tested up to 4 GB; with the OS 1.30+ filesystem improvements, cards up to 64 GB work reliably. Use FAT32. Avoid SD-to-CF adapters in long-term use — many introduce write failures the OT silently tolerates until a save corrupts. Genuine industrial CF cards (SanDisk Extreme, Lexar Professional 1066x or higher) remain available and worth the purchase.

5 · MIDI Thru

5-pin DIN. Echoes incoming MIDI from the IN port without re-quantising or modifying it.

6 · MIDI Out

5-pin DIN. Sends data from the OT's eight MIDI tracks plus clock, transport, and SysEx. Supports Elektron's TurboMIDI protocol — higher-than-standard baud rates between two Elektron boxes for faster CC streams. Anything connected, Elektron or otherwise, falls back to standard MIDI baud automatically.

7 · MIDI In

5-pin DIN. Receives clock, notes, CCs, NRPNs, and SysEx. Used for slaving the OT to an external clock, for triggering audio tracks from a keyboard, and for receiving real-time controller data into the eight MIDI tracks.

8 · Input C / D

Two ¼″ jacks forming the CD pair. C is the top jack (left channel), D is the bottom jack (right channel). On MK1 the inputs are unbalanced at +8 dBu peak. (The MK2 changed this to impedance-balanced inputs at +17 dBu, a 9 dB headroom improvement that is the single most-cited reason to prefer MK2 for studio use.)

9 · Input A / B

Two ¼″ jacks forming the AB pair. A is the top jack (left channel), B is the bottom jack (right channel). Same electrical characteristics as the CD pair. Together with CD, these provide four mono input channels arranged as two stereo pairs.

10 · Cue Out L / R

Stacked vertical pair (L on top, R on bottom). Impedance- balanced ¼″. By default the CUE bus carries whatever tracks are currently routed to it via CUE+TRACK — useful for headphone pre-listen. With Studio Mode enabled (PROJECT → CTRL → AUDIO → OUTPUTS = STUDIO) the CUE bus becomes a completely independent main pair: each track gets a MAIN volume and a CUE volume, and the two pairs can carry different mixes for front-of-house and stage monitoring, or for a hardware mixer return.

11 · Main Out L / R

Stacked vertical pair (L on top, R on bottom), to the right of Cue Out. Impedance-balanced ¼″. Carries the sum of all tracks routed to MAIN, after the master track's FX if a Master machine is loaded on T8.

12 · Headphones output

Single ¼″ stereo jack at the right edge. Driven by the front-panel headphones-volume knob (callout 1 in chapter 4). Mirrors the CUE bus by default; mirrors the MAIN pair when the CUE bus is empty and Studio Mode is off.

Warning · ground hum on unbalanced inputs

The MK1's unbalanced inputs are susceptible to ground loops when the Octatrack and a connected synth or mixer are on different mains circuits. If you hear 50/60 Hz hum that disappears when you unplug the source, you have a ground loop. The right fix is a properly grounded power distribution; a serviceable temporary fix is a transformer-isolated DI box on the offending source.

A small note on the chassis

The MK1's enclosure is silver-painted steel with the rear panel forming a structural chassis. Two screws on each side and a row along the rear edge open the unit. Inside, three ribbon cables connect the upper UI PCB to the main board — significant if you ever need to service the crossfader or a failed encoder. The MK2 uses two ribbons and a slightly different geometry; service docs from MK2 do not all transfer.

Battery: a CR2450 lithium-manganese cell, retained in a clip on the main PCB. It backs up RAM-held pattern and part data so the unit boots into the project it was last working on without a full CF reload — Elektron quotes a six-year shelf life under typical use. When the battery fails, the Octatrack will still work, but every cold start will take longer and any unsaved work in RAM will be lost. Replacement is straightforward and detailed in Appendix G.

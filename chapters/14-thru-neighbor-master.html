Thru, Neighbor & Master machines

Three machines that don't play samples. Thru passes external audio through; Neighbor reads the previous track's signal; Master turns a track into a master-bus processor.

Thru machine

A Thru machine routes external audio (from one of the rear-panel input pairs) directly into the track's signal chain. The track becomes a live audio processor — your synth or external source runs through the OT's PLAYBACK, AMP, FX1, and FX2.

Thru-machine PLAYBACK page has different parameters: source selection (AB, CD, A, B, C, D), input gain, balance.

Use cases

  • Treat the OT as a multi-effects unit for your synth
  • Apply scenes/p-locks to live input — modulate the FX on external sound through the crossfader
  • Use a Thru-machine track as a "drone layer" running an external oscillator through the OT's filter and reverb

A Thru track does not have a sample, but you can still place trigs on it — they'll just have no audible effect (no sample to play). The trigs can carry parameter locks, however, which means you can sequence FX changes on the Thru-track's input without the input itself being sequenced.

Neighbor machine

A Neighbor machine reads the audio output of the previous track (T2's neighbor is T1's output, T3's neighbor is T2's, etc.). It feeds that audio into its own signal chain — meaning T2's PLAYBACK /AMP/FX1/FX2 process T1's output.

This creates serial effect chains. T1 plays a sample; T2 (Neighbor) adds a delay; T3 (Neighbor of T2) adds a reverb. Each track contributes one effects pair, but the audio cascades.

Why this matters

The Octatrack normally limits you to 2 effects per track. With Neighbor machines you can stack effects across multiple tracks — 4, 6, even 8 effects in series.

Cost: each Neighbor track consumes a track slot. If you want T1 with a 6-effect chain, you'll spend T1, T2, T3 on the chain. The upside is full p-lock control over each effect stage independently.

Wraparound

T1's neighbor is T8 — the chain wraps. This is rarely useful but occasionally produces feedback paths if used carelessly (e.g. placing T8 as Master with T1 as Neighbor of T8 creates a feedback loop). The OT prevents true feedback through Neighbor, but the wraparound is real.

Tip · Neighbor for parallel processing

Place the same sample on T1 and a Neighbor on T2. The two tracks now play the sample with independent FX chains. Mix them on the mixer page for parallel processing — wet/dry blends, frequency splits, etc. T1 = clean, T2 = filtered + delayed. Or T2 muted, crossfader-controlled to fade in.

Master machine

Loaded only on T8. Turns T8 into a master-bus processor: T8's sample slot is ignored, T8's trigs are silent, but T8's FX1 and FX2 process the entire MAIN bus sum (the mix of T1 through T7, plus any tracks routed to MAIN).

T8's Master configuration:

  • FX1 — typically a Compressor or EQ for master-bus glue
  • FX2 — typically a final EQ, limiter, or Lo-Fi for character
  • AMP volume on T8 — acts as master output volume

What you lose

You lose T8 as an audio-producing track. Eight tracks becomes seven. For most projects this is a fair trade for master-bus FX. For projects already at 7-track capacity, consider whether master FX are worth the dedicated slot.

What you gain

  • Master compression for unified loudness across the mix
  • Master EQ for tonal correction
  • Lo-fi or saturation for character ("OT mix glue")
  • Scene control over master parameters — fade out the entire mix via a scene-locked AMP volume

Choosing your machine layout

A few canonical setups:

Studio mix layout

  • T1–T6: Static and Flex playback (drums, bass, leads, etc.)
  • T7: spare Flex track for live capture
  • T8: Master machine

Live performance layout

  • T1: Pickup machine — live looper
  • T2–T6: Flex playback for sequenced material
  • T7: Thru machine — external synth input
  • T8: Master

Effects-rack layout

  • T1: Thru machine — input
  • T2–T7: Neighbor chain — six stages of effects
  • T8: Master

These are starting points. The OT rewards experimenting with machine assignments — a project's layout is part of its sound design.

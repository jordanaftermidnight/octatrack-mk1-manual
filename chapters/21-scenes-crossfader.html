Scenes & the crossfader

The Octatrack's signature performance feature. Scenes are parameter snapshots; the crossfader morphs between two of them in real time. This is what makes the OT a "performance sampler" and not just a sequencer.

Scenes — what they are

A scene is a snapshot of parameter values across all eight tracks. Specifically, it stores any parameter that has been scene-locked — meaning explicitly assigned to that scene with a particular value. Parameters that are not scene-locked don't appear in the scene; they remain controlled by the part's base values and pattern p-locks.

Each part has 16 scenes, numbered 1–16. The crossfader assigns one scene to its A end and one to its B end. Moving the fader morphs every scene-locked parameter between its A value and its B value.

The fader is a parameter morph, not an audio gate

Repeating from chapter 8: the crossfader does not gate audio. It interpolates parameter values. If you want it to fade a track in or out, you scene-lock the track's level — at zero in scene A and at maximum in scene B. The fader then becomes a level interpolator for that track.

The implication: every parameter that supports scene-locking can be morphed by the fader. Pitch, FX cutoff, sample slot, scene locks themselves — anything. This is why the crossfader is more than a DJ-style mixer fader.

Setting a scene lock

  1. Hold SCENE A (or SCENE B) and press a trig key to select which scene to assign to that fader end.
  2. Make sure the fader is fully at the chosen end (A or B).
  3. Hold SCENE A (or B) again and turn a knob on any parameter page. The knob's value is recorded into that scene.
  4. Release. The parameter is now scene-locked.

The locked parameter shows a small "X" indicator on the LCD when the scene is active. Move the fader: the parameter follows the interpolation between A and B.

What can be scene-locked

Almost every parameter on the OT supports scene-locking:

  • All PLAYBACK parameters (pitch, slot, start, length, rate)
  • All AMP parameters (envelope, volume, balance, XVOL)
  • All FX1 / FX2 parameters
  • LFO depths and destinations
  • Track mute states (binary scene-lock — track on at A, off at B)

Multi-track scenes are powerful: scene 1 might lock T1 mute on, T2 cutoff at 50, T3 reverb mix at 100; scene 2 locks T1 mute off, T2 cutoff at 127, T3 reverb mix at 0. Sliding the fader morphs all three simultaneously.

SCENE A vs SCENE B (the buttons)

The Scene A / Scene B buttons at the bottom-right are assigners. They don't trigger scenes — they choose which scene the corresponding fader end maps to. The actual morph happens via the fader.

Held alone, each turns the trig row into a 16-scene selector for its fader end. Press a trig to assign that scene to that end. With FUNCTION they become MUTE buttons — instantly silencing the assigned scene without moving the fader.

Crossfader assignments and patterns

Scene assignments are part-level. Switching parts changes which scenes are mapped. Switching patterns within the same part preserves the scene assignment but the patterns play different sequencer content — meaning your scene-controlled morph stays the same even as the underlying pattern changes.

Recipe — basic build/drop

Recipe — energy curve via crossfader

Setup:

  • Scene A: T1 (drums) muted, T2 (bass) cutoff at 30, FX2 reverb mix at 0, T6 (atmosphere) volume at 100
  • Scene B: T1 (drums) unmuted, T2 (bass) cutoff at 127, FX2 reverb mix at 60, T6 (atmosphere) volume at 0

Move the fader from A to B over 16 bars. The track builds: drums fade in, bass opens up, reverb increases, atmosphere drops out. One fader gesture, full energy curve.

Recipe — A/B variation

Scene A is the "main" mix. Scene B is the "variation" mix — different drum slot, different bass pitch, different FX. Move the fader to instantly transform the pattern. Useful for live A/B section switches without changing patterns.

The classic crossfader transition

A widely-used technique: prepare two patterns that share a part. Pattern A's scene 1 is the current state; pattern B's scene 1 is the destination state. As you switch from A to B (chained pattern), the fader can slide simultaneously to interpolate. Result: smooth tempo-locked transitions between patterns that don't sound like sudden cuts.

Hidden · scene 17 — the "current" scene

Scenes 1–16 are user-assignable. There's an implicit "scene 17" — the current state of all parameters when no scene is active. Pressing FUNCTION + SCENE A clears the A assignment and the fader becomes A=current/B=current — meaning the fader has no effect. Useful for temporarily disabling crossfader morphing without un-assigning your scenes.

Parts and scene scope

Each part has its own 16 scenes. Switching parts (within a bank, via the Part menu) loads a different set of scenes. This means a bank's four parts can each have completely different crossfader-driven morphs — switch parts, and your fader gesture means something different.

Carefully designed parts + scenes + patterns is how OT sets are built. A live performer might have 16 patterns × 4 parts × 16 scenes = 1024 distinct addressable musical states per bank. With 16 banks per project, the combinatorial space is enormous.
